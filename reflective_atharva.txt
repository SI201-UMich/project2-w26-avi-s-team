Reflective Questions

I do not think the current system provides adequate accountability around short-term rentals in San Francisco. 
On paper, it sounds strong because multiple parties are involved: hosts must register, Airbnb must display registration 
information, and third parties can scrape listings and report possible violations. In practice, though, this setup puts too much 
responsibility on outside observers instead of the platform and the city. If enforcement depends on independent researchers 
scraping listings and filing complaints, then accountability is reactive rather than built into the system. It also allows 
hosts and platforms to benefit from weak enforcement unless someone else takes the time to investigate. From the perspective 
of the Software Engineering Code of Ethics, this seems inadequate because software professionals are supposed to act in the 
public interest and ensure their products meet high professional standards. A better system would require Airbnb to automatically 
verify registration numbers directly with the city before a listing can go live, rather than merely displaying whatever 
information a host enters. That would shift accountability toward prevention instead of after-the-fact detection and would better 
protect neighborhoods, tenants, and the public.

One important factor to consider when discussing the legality of web scraping is whether the data is publicly accessible or 
whether access restrictions were bypassed. This matters because recent U.S. cases have suggested that scraping truly public data 
is treated differently from accessing password-protected or blocked systems. In Van Buren v. United States, the Supreme Court 
narrowed the scope of the Computer Fraud and Abuse Act by focusing on whether someone accessed areas of a computer that were 
actually off-limits, not just whether they used information for an improper purpose. In the hiQ v. LinkedIn litigation, courts 
also treated scraping of public LinkedIn profiles differently from hacking into private data, even though other claims such as 
breach of contract could still apply. That distinction is important because it shows that legality is not simply about whether 
scraping happens, but about how the scraping is done, what protections are in place, and whether the scraper ignored technical 
or contractual barriers. To me, this is one of the most important factors because it separates scraping that gathers open public
information from scraping that resembles unauthorized intrusion.

When deciding whether web scraping is ethical, I think at least two guidelines are essential. First, the scraper should consider
whether people reasonably expected their data to be collected and reused in that way, even if the information was technically
public. The Cambridge Analytica scandal showed how data can be harvested and repurposed on a massive scale without meaningful
consent, producing serious harms for users and public trust. Clearview AI raises a similar concern because it built a 
facial-recognition database from huge numbers of online images, which many people never expected would be turned into a
surveillance tool. Second, the scraper should evaluate whether the social benefit outweighs the risk of harm. Scraping
can support accountability, journalism, and research, but it can also enable surveillance, manipulation, and privacy
violations. I would add that data collection should be minimized to only what is necessary for the public-interest goal,
and the collected information should not be reused for unrelated purposes. In other words, ethical scraping should be 
transparent, limited, and clearly justified, not based on the idea that “public” automatically means “fair game.”
